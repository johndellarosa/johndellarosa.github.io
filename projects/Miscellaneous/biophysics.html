Enthalpy and the Thermodynamic Definition of Entropy

By the differential form of the first law of Thermodynamics, we have $$dU=(\frac{\partial U}{\partial S})_{V,N}dS+(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V})_{S,N}dV=TdS-p_{ext}dV$$ *Negative sign for pressure since our dV is pointing in opposite direction of pext $$H\equiv U+PV$$ $$dH=dU+d(PV)=dU+(pdV+Vdp)$$ $$=(TdS-pdV)+pdV+Vdp$$ $$=TdS+Vdp$$ If we are working in a constant temperature environment, we can let dp=0, yielding the formula $$dH=TdS=dq$$ $$dS=\frac{dq_{rev}}{T}$$ This is the so-called "Thermodynamic definition of Entropy" which holds for a reversible process at constant pressure
However, what about for irreversible processes? The first law still holds such that $$U=q+w$$ but $$q_{rev}\neq q_{irr}$$ and $$w_{rev}\neq w_{irr}$$ A more general statement which holds for irreversible processes is $$dS\geq \frac{\delta Q}{T}$$ This is known as the Clausius Inequality.
