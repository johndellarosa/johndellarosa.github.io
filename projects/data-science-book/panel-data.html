Introduction

Panel Data refers to datasets that contain observations of multiple entities (such as individuals, firms, countries) over multiple time periods. Unlike purely cross-sectional or time-series data, panel data captures both inter-individual (cross-sectional) and intra-individual (time-series) variations.

Balanced

  • Balanced Panel: Every entity is observed in every time period
  • Unbalanced Panel: Entities are not observed in all time periods, leading to gaps in the data

General Structure

Panel data has two indices:
  • Entity index (i): Denotes the individual units.
  • Time Index (t): Denotes the time periods.
A general panel data model can be expressed as: $$Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\beta X_{it}+\varepsilon_{it}$$
  • \(Y_{it}\) is the dependent variable for entity i at time t.
  • \(\alpha_{i}\) represents entity-specific effects
  • \(X_{it}\) is a vector of independent variables.
  • \(\varepsilon_{it}\) is the error term.

Pooled OLS

Pooled OLS assumes no entity-specific effects and is the simplest panel model. $$Y_{it}+\beta_{0}+\beta_{1}X_{it}+\varepsilon$$

Assumptions

  • No unobserved heterogeneity \((\alpha_i=0)\)
  • Error terms \(\varepsilon_{it}\) are i.i.d.
  • No serial correlation or heteroskedasticity

Pros

Simplicity and ease of implementation

Cons

Biased and inconsistent estimates if unobserved heterogeneity is present and correlated with regressors.

Fixed Effects Model

$$Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\beta X_{it}+\varepsilon$$

Estimation Methods

  • De-meaning
  • Dummy Variable Approach: Including entity-specific dummy variables in the regression

Assumptions

  • \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon|X_{it},\alpha_i]=0\)
  • \(\alpha_i\) may be correlated with \(X_{it}\)

Pros

  • Controls for all time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity
  • Consistent estimates even if \(\alpha_i\) is correlated with regressors.

Cons

  • Cannot estimate the effects of time-invariant variables
  • Potential loss of degrees of freedom with many entities

Random Effects Model

$$Y_{it}=\alpha+\beta X_{it}+u_i +\varepsilon$$

Estimation Method

Generalized Least Squares

Assumptions

  • \(u_i\) is uncorrelated with \(X_{it}\) across all entities and time periods
  • \(\mathbb{E}[u_i]=0,\quad \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_{it}=0\)

Pros

  • More efficient than Fixed Effect Model if the Random Effect assumptions hold
  • Can estimate time-invariant variables

Cons

  • Biased and inconsistent estimates if \(u_i\) is correlated with regressors

Dynamic Panel Models

$$Y_{it}=\alpha Y_{it-1}+\beta X_{it}+\varepsilon_{it}$$

Estimation Methods

  • Arellano-Bond Estimator: Uses lagged levels as instruments for differenced equations
  • System Generalized Method of Moments: Combines equations in levels and differences for more efficient estimates

Assumptions

  • No serial correlation in error terms
  • Validity of instruments (no correlation with error terms)

Pros

  • Captures dynamic relationships and inertia in the dependent variable
  • Addresses endogeneity of lagged dependent variables

Cons

  • Requires strong instruments, which may lead to finite sample biases
  • Complex to implement and validate
